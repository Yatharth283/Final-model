Chapter 2: Acids, Bases, and Salts

Introduction

Acids, bases, and salts are fundamental chemical substances with widespread applications in our daily lives. Understanding their properties and reactions is crucial in chemistry.

Acids

Acids are substances that release **hydrogen ions (H⁺)** in aqueous solutions. They have a **sour taste** and turn **blue litmus paper red**.

Properties of Acids

  • Acids have a **pH value less than 7**.
  • They react with metals to produce **hydrogen gas**.
  • They react with carbonates to produce **carbon dioxide gas**.
  • Strong acids can be **corrosive**.

Bases

Bases are substances that release **hydroxide ions (OH⁻)** in solution. They have a **bitter taste**, feel **soapy**, and turn **red litmus paper blue**.

Properties of Bases

  • Bases have a **pH value greater than 7**.
  • They feel **slippery** to touch.
  • They react with acids to form **salt and water** (Neutralization Reaction).

Neutralization Reaction

When an acid reacts with a base, they neutralize each other to form **salt and water**.

Example: HCl (acid) + NaOH (base) → NaCl (salt) + H₂O (water)

Salts

Salts are formed from the reaction between acids and bases. They have various applications in daily life.

Common Salts and Their Uses

  • Sodium Chloride (NaCl): Common salt used in cooking.
  • Sodium Carbonate (Na₂CO₃): Used in washing soda.
  • Calcium Sulfate (CaSO₄·2H₂O): Used in making plaster of Paris.
  • Ammonium Nitrate (NH₄NO₃): Used in fertilizers.

pH and Its Importance

The **pH scale** measures the acidity or basicity of a substance, ranging from **0 to 14**.

  • pH **less than 7** → Acidic solution.
  • pH **equal to 7** → Neutral solution.
  • pH **greater than 7** → Basic solution.

Importance of pH in Daily Life

  • Soil pH: Determines crop growth; acidic soil requires lime treatment.
  • Human Body: Blood pH remains around 7.4 for proper functioning.
  • Tooth Decay: pH below 5.5 in the mouth can cause cavities.

Water of Crystallization

Some salts contain water molecules in their crystalline form, known as **water of crystallization**.

Example: Gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) contains two molecules of water.

Plaster of Paris

Plaster of Paris (POP) is obtained by heating gypsum.

Reaction: CaSO₄·2H₂O → CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O

It is used in making statues, molds, and medical bandages.

Bleaching Powder

Bleaching powder (CaOCl₂) is used as a disinfectant and in the textile industry.
