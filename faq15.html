Chapter 15 - Our Environment

1. What is an ecosystem? Explain its components.

An ecosystem is a biological community where living organisms interact with each other and their non-living environment. It includes two main components:

  • Biotic Components: These are the living organisms in an ecosystem, including producers (plants), consumers (animals), and decomposers (bacteria and fungi).
  • Abiotic Components: These include non-living factors such as sunlight, air, water, temperature, and minerals that influence the ecosystem.

2. Explain the concept of the food chain with an example.

A food chain is a sequence that shows how energy and nutrients flow from one organism to another. It consists of different trophic levels:

Example:

  • Grass (Producer) → Grasshopper (Primary Consumer) → Frog (Secondary Consumer) → Snake (Tertiary Consumer) → Eagle (Top Predator)

3. What is a food web? How is it different from a food chain?

A food web is a complex network of multiple interconnected food chains. It represents various feeding relationships in an ecosystem, unlike a linear food chain, which shows a single pathway of energy flow.

4. Why are decomposers important for the environment?

Decomposers like bacteria and fungi break down dead organisms and organic waste, recycling nutrients back into the ecosystem. This process maintains soil fertility and keeps the environment clean.

5. What are trophic levels? Explain with an example.

Trophic levels are the different stages in a food chain, where each level represents an organism's position in the energy flow.

  • Producers: Plants and algae (Trophic Level 1)
  • Primary Consumers: Herbivores like rabbits (Trophic Level 2)
  • Secondary Consumers: Carnivores like snakes (Trophic Level 3)
  • Tertiary Consumers: Top predators like eagles (Trophic Level 4)

6. What happens to the energy as we move up the trophic levels?

According to the 10% law, only 10% of the energy is transferred to the next trophic level, while 90% is lost as heat. This limits the number of trophic levels in a food chain.

7. What are biodegradable and non-biodegradable substances? Give examples.

Biodegradable Substances: These decompose naturally by microorganisms. Examples: Vegetable peels, paper.

Non-Biodegradable Substances: These do not decompose easily and remain in the environment for years. Examples: Plastic, glass.

8. How do human activities affect the environment?

Human activities like deforestation, pollution, and excessive use of resources lead to climate change, habitat destruction, and loss of biodiversity.

9. What is biological magnification? Explain with an example.

Biological magnification refers to the accumulation of toxic substances, such as pesticides, in organisms at higher trophic levels. Example: DDT in aquatic food chains affects fish-eating birds.

10. What are ozone layer depletion and its causes?

The ozone layer protects Earth from harmful UV rays. The release of CFCs (chlorofluorocarbons) from refrigerators and air conditioners damages this layer, increasing health risks like skin cancer.

11. Why should we use eco-friendly products?

Eco-friendly products reduce pollution, minimize waste, and help conserve natural resources, ensuring a sustainable future.

12. How can we manage waste effectively?

Effective waste management includes the 3Rs: Reduce, Reuse, and Recycle. Proper segregation of waste into biodegradable and non-biodegradable categories helps in efficient disposal and recycling.
