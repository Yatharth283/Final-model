Electricity - Chapter 12

Atomic Structure

An atom consists of a nucleus containing protons and neutrons, with electrons revolving around it in discrete energy levels.

Electrons are responsible for electrical conduction in materials, especially in metals.

Valence Electrons and Electric Current

Valence electrons are the outermost electrons in an atom. In metals, these electrons are loosely bound and move freely, forming an electric current when a potential difference is applied.

The movement of these free electrons constitutes electric current in a conductor.

Charge

Charge is the fundamental property of matter, measured in coulombs (C). An electron has a charge of -1.6 × 10⁻¹⁹ C.

Like charges repel each other, while opposite charges attract.

Conductors and Insulators

Conductors, such as copper and aluminum, allow the easy flow of electric current due to free electrons.

Insulators, such as rubber and glass, have tightly bound electrons and resist the flow of current.

Electric Potential and Potential Difference

Electric potential is defined as the work done per unit charge in bringing a charge from infinity to a specific point.

Formula: V = W/Q, where V is potential, W is work done, and Q is charge.

Potential difference between two points drives current through a circuit.

Electric Current (I)

Electric current is the rate of flow of charge through a conductor.

Formula: I = Q/t, where I is current, Q is charge, and t is time.

SI unit of current is Ampere (A).

Drift Velocity of Electrons

Drift velocity is the average velocity attained by free electrons in a conductor due to an electric field.

Formula: v_d = I / (nAq), where n is the number of charge carriers per unit volume, A is the cross-sectional area, and q is the charge of an electron.

Battery and Its Working

A battery consists of electrochemical cells that convert chemical energy into electrical energy.

It maintains a potential difference that drives the flow of electrons.

Electric Circuit

An electric circuit is a closed path through which electric current flows.

It consists of components such as resistors, capacitors, and voltage sources.

Resistance and Ohm’s Law

Ohm’s Law states that the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.

Formula: V = IR, where R is resistance.

Factors Affecting Resistance

Resistance depends on material, length, temperature, and cross-sectional area.

Formula: R = ρl/A, where ρ is resistivity.

Ohmic and Non-Ohmic Resistors

Ohmic resistors follow Ohm’s Law (e.g., metals), while non-ohmic resistors (e.g., diodes) do not.

Superconductors

Superconductors have zero resistance below a critical temperature.

Combination of Resistors

Series: R_eq = R₁ + R₂ + ...

Parallel: 1/R_eq = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + ...

EMF and Terminal Voltage

Electromotive Force (EMF) is the potential difference when no current flows.

Terminal voltage is the potential difference when current flows.

Heating Effect of Current (Joule’s Law)

Heat energy produced: H = I²Rt

Uses of Heating Effect

Electric irons, geysers, room heaters, fuses, etc.

Electric Power

Formula: P = VI, P = I²R, P = V²/R

Unit: Watt (W). 1 kWh = 3.6 × 10⁶ J.
